## Key Observations

### 1. Thinking mode invalidates A2 and B
Qwen3-235B switches between ultra-short vanilla responses (4 words: just the answer) and 6000–11000 word extended thinking responses. `words_if_atomic` (150–200) cannot be compared to either extreme. A2=18.87 and B=3.75 are **artifacts of thinking mode**, not real word estimation calibration.

### 2. Worst answer calibration of all arms (D)
D_hch=0.639, D_van=0.786 — worst of all models run so far. Qwen reports p_correct=0.95–1.00 on nearly every answer regardless of correctness. Extreme systematic overconfidence.

### 3. Always decomposes (12/12)
Like Haiku, Qwen3 decomposes every question. But paid off only 1/12 (Q55: TC⁰ correctly identified via HCH). Interestingly this is the question Haiku refused to answer.

### 4. Q48: Off by one digit
HCH answer = 5429515550378, gold = 5429515560378. Off by 10000. Close but not close enough.

### 5. Q53 vanilla unique case
Vanilla correctly answered 0, but reported p_correct=0.00 (perfect anti-confidence). This is a scoring edge case: judge=True but p_correct=0.0 means Brier=1.0 for D_van on this question. Qwen was "accidentally right" with no confidence.

### 6. Q43 truncation again
HCH response cut off (answer=None) — same pattern as Haiku and v1. Q43 appears to be a systematically hard truncation case.

---

## Comparison with Haiku (floor model)
Both Qwen3-235B and Haiku achieved identical accuracy (1/12 HCH, 1/12 vanilla = 8.3%). Despite being a 235B parameter model with extended thinking, Qwen3 does not outperform Haiku on HLE-12. This is likely because HLE-12 questions are genuinely hard and Qwen3's thinking mode produces high word-count responses but not better final answers.
