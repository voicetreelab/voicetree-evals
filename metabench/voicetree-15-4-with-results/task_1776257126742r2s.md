═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
PROBLEM FRAMING (important — read first)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Haiku scored #2 on composite Brier (0.55) despite 12.5% actual accuracy. This surfaced a reward-hacking concern: pure Brier rewards "uniform pessimism" — a model that predicts low confidence on everything gets good calibration for free when it's bad at everything.

The fix is to **decompose Brier into Calibration − Resolution + Uncertainty** and report resolution separately. A model with flat predictions (Haiku's likely profile) has near-zero resolution even when overall Brier looks good.

Brier decomposition (Murphy 1973):
  Brier = Reliability (calibration error) − Resolution (discrimination power) + Uncertainty (base-rate variance)

  Reliability = Σₖ (nₖ/N) × (p̄ₖ − ōₖ)²    — how close bin-mean-prediction is to bin-mean-outcome (lower better)
  Resolution  = Σₖ (nₖ/N) × (ōₖ − ō)²      — how much bin-mean-outcomes differ from overall base rate (higher better)
  Uncertainty = ō × (1 − ō)                — variance of outcomes (fixed for a given dataset)

**For small N**: binning is noisy. Also compute **AUC of p_correct vs actual correctness** as a rank-based discrimination proxy. AUC=0.5 is chance; AUC=1 is perfect discrimination.
